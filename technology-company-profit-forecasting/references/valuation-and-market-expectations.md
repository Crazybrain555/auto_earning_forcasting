# Valuation and Market Expectations

Valuation translates operating economics and capital allocation into expected
return. It is not a multiple pasted onto an earnings estimate.

## Reported, normalized and owner-cash views

Use the reported, normalized and owner-cash views defined in
`earnings-power-and-mean-reversion.md`. Reconcile reported GAAP or IFRS results
to a normalized operating view and to an owner-cash view. Adjustments must be
line-specific, sourced and reversible. Stock compensation, restructuring,
acquisition accounting, tax benefits, leases, capitalized development, recurring
acquisition spend and dilution cannot be removed merely because management calls
them non-GAAP.

Normalize only to expose economics. Always retain the reported view and show
the value effect of disputed adjustments.

## Value-creation schedule

For each historical and forecast period calculate:

- NOPAT and average invested capital;
- ROIC and incremental ROIC;
- organic and acquired reinvestment;
- reinvestment rate and fundamental growth;
- free cash flow and conversion;
- ROIC minus required return;
- explicit fade in growth, margins, incremental returns and reinvestment.

Growth is valuable only when incremental returns exceed the required return.
Fundamental growth must reconcile, directionally and arithmetically, to
reinvestment rate × incremental ROIC. If this identity does not fit the
reported business, identify hidden economic investment, acquisition effects,
working-capital funding or measurement limitations.

## Explicit fade

Do not assume either permanent excess returns or automatic mean reversion.
Model the mechanism:

- competitive entry and capacity response;
- product maturity, mix and price;
- customer bargaining or concentration;
- switching, qualification, ecosystem or regulatory barriers;
- replacement versus growth demand;
- required reinvestment and capital intensity.

State the year, level and evidence for steady-state margins, growth,
incremental ROIC and reinvestment. The red team tests the weakest persistence
claim.

## Primary valuation methods

Choose the primary method whose accounting and economic assumptions fit the
issuer. Discounted cash flow from the integrated statements is often useful;
residual-income valuation can add an independent accounting view when clean
surplus and book-value bridges are supportable. Add a second method only when
it contributes distinct information rather than satisfying a method count.

Use normalized earnings power, replacement economics or comparable multiples
as cross-checks when economically appropriate. Multiples must specify the
denominator's accounting basis, cycle state, growth, return and leverage; peer
similarity by sector label is insufficient.

When both DCF and residual income are used, reconcile them. A material difference usually indicates a
book-value, terminal, clean-surplus, discount-rate or accounting bridge issue.
Do not average conflicting outputs before diagnosing the difference.

## Terminal constraints

Terminal value must satisfy all of the following:

- discount rate exceeds perpetual nominal growth;
- growth is consistent with reinvestment and steady-state incremental ROIC;
- margin and return assumptions include plausible competitive response;
- capital intensity, working capital and maintenance investment persist;
- dilution, debt, pensions, leases, minorities and non-operating assets are
  bridged consistently.

Show the portion of enterprise value attributable to the terminal period and
the operating assumptions required to support it.

## Enterprise-to-equity and per-share bridge

Bridge enterprise value to common equity using dated net debt, leases,
minorities, investments, pensions, tax assets or liabilities and other
non-operating claims.  Use valuation-date fully diluted shares: valuation-date
basic shares plus options, awards and convertibles that are economically
dilutive under the stated valuation basis, adjusted for dated buybacks and
issuance.  This point-in-time denominator is neither ending basic shares nor
the period weighted-average diluted denominator used for GAAP EPS. Value per
share must be formula-linked to this bridge.

## Reverse-implied expectations

Starting from market enterprise or equity value, solve for named operating
drivers, not a generic optimism score. Examples include units, share, ASP,
gross profit per unit, steady-state margin, incremental ROIC, reinvestment,
fade duration or commercialization probability.

Compare the implied path with:

- physical capacity and end demand;
- customer economics and competitive response;
- historical and peer unit economics;
- technology and qualification gates;
- balance-sheet and cash requirements.

The result is a disagreement map: what the market must believe, what the
research case believes, which observation will resolve the difference, and
how much value depends on it.

## Required return, margin of safety and posture

Required return and margin of safety are explicit analyst judgments. State the
currency, inflation basis, leverage, liquidity, business fragility and scenario
risks considered. Never conceal risk by increasing a discount rate while also
lowering operating forecasts for the same event without explaining the double
count.

For the reference path show value per share, expected return, downside, time
horizon and first failure point. When a supported probabilistic joint-scenario
set is active, show probabilities and the same outputs for each material rival
state. Bear/Base/Bull are optional labels. The investment posture follows
from expected return and downside relative to the declared hurdle and safety
margin; it is not a qualitative score.
